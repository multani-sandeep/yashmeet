### Question 4 — AC vs DC and Circuit Calculations (8 marks)

**(a)** Award **1 mark each** for three of:

- **DC (direct current)**: current flows in **one direction only**; the magnitude may be constant or varying but never reverses. **[1]**
- **AC (alternating current)**: the current **periodically reverses direction**; in the UK mains it does so 50 times per second (50 Hz). **[1]**
- Source of DC: a **battery / cell / solar cell**. **[1]**
- Source of AC: **UK mains supply / generator / power station**. **[1]** *(max 3)*

**[3]**

---

**(b)**

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{50} = 0.02 \text{ s}$$

- Correct answer $T = 0.02$ s (accept $20$ ms) **[2]**

*(Award 1 mark for correct substitution if arithmetic error in final answer.)*

**[2]**

---

**(c)** Award **1 mark each** for three of:

- Normally, all current that flows out through the live wire returns through the neutral wire, so both carry the same current. **[1]**
- If a person touches the live wire, current can flow through their body to earth / ground via their feet. **[1]**
- This additional path means more current leaves through live than returns through neutral — creating a **current imbalance**. **[1]**
- The RCD detects this imbalance and breaks the circuit, protecting the person from sustained electrocution. **[1]** *(max 3)*

### Question 5 — Nature of Sound Waves (10 marks)

**(a)** Award **1 mark each**:

- **Transverse**: particle oscillation is **perpendicular** to the direction of wave travel / energy transfer. **[1]**
- **Longitudinal**: particle oscillation is **parallel** (along the same line) to the direction of wave travel. **[1]**
- Sound is **longitudinal** — the air particles vibrate back and forth in the same direction as the sound travels, creating compressions and rarefactions. **[1]**

**[3]**

---

**(b)** Award **1 mark each**:

- Sound requires a medium (particles) to travel — it is transmitted by particles colliding with their neighbours and passing on the vibration. **[1]**
- In a vacuum there are no particles, so there is nothing to vibrate and transmit the wave. **[1]**

**[2]**

---

**(c)**

Total distance travelled by sound $= 2 \times 680 = 1360$ m (sound goes to the wall and back).

$$v = \frac{d}{t} = \frac{1360}{4.0} = 340 \text{ m s}^{-1}$$

- Correct recognition that total distance $= 2 \times 680 = 1360$ m **[1]**
- Correct use of $v = d/t$ **[1]**
- Correct answer $v = 340$ m s$^{-1}$ **[1]**
- Comment: the calculated value exactly matches the accepted value of 340 m s$^{-1}$ / consistent with the accepted value **[1]**

**[4]**

---

**(d)** Particles in a solid are much **closer together / more tightly bonded** than in a gas, so vibrations are transmitted more readily from one particle to the next — the wave travels faster. **[1]**

**[1]**

### Question 6 — Wave Properties: Frequency, Wavelength and the Wave Equation (12 marks)

**(a)(i)** Frequency: the number of complete waves (oscillations) passing a point **per second**; unit: Hz (hertz). **[1]**

*(Must include: per second or "per unit time".)*

---

**(a)(ii)** Wavelength: the distance between **two adjacent points in phase** on the wave (e.g. crest to crest, or compression to compression); the length of one complete wave. **[1]**

---

**(a)(iii)** Time period: the time taken for **one complete oscillation / one complete wave** to pass a point; $T = 1/f$. **[1]**

---

**(b)(i)**

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{340}{850} = 0.40 \text{ m}$$

- Correct rearrangement of $v = f\lambda$ **[1]**
- Correct answer $\lambda = 0.40$ m **[1]**

**[2]**

---

**(b)(ii)**

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{850} = 1.18 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s} \approx 1.2 \text{ ms}$$

- Correct answer $T \approx 1.18 \times 10^{-3}$ s (accept $1.2 \times 10^{-3}$ s or $1.18$ ms) **[2]**

*(Award 1 mark for correct method if arithmetic slip.)*

**[2]**

---

**(c)(i)** The frequency **stays the same** (unchanged). Reason: frequency is determined by the source of the sound, not the medium. **[1]**

**[1]**

---

**(c)(ii)**

$$\lambda_\text{water} = \frac{v_\text{water}}{f} = \frac{1500}{850} = 1.76 \text{ m (3 s.f.)}$$

- Correct method using new speed with same frequency **[1]**
- Correct answer $\lambda_\text{water} \approx 1.76$ m (accept $1.8$ m; ECF from (c)(i)) **[1]**

**[2]**

---

**(d)** Award **1 mark each**:

- Higher frequency → higher pitch; lower frequency → lower pitch. Frequency is the rate of vibration, and a faster vibration is perceived as a higher-pitched sound. **[1]**
- Larger amplitude → louder sound; smaller amplitude → quieter sound. Amplitude is the maximum displacement of the particle; larger displacement carries more energy, producing a louder sound. **[1]**

### Question 8 — Ultrasound and Wave Behaviour (8 marks)

**(a)(i)**

Total distance = $v \times t = 1500 \times 0.24 = 360$ m.

This is the distance to the seabed and back, so:

$$\text{Depth} = \frac{360}{2} = 180 \text{ m}$$

- Correct calculation of total distance $= 360$ m **[1]**
- Correct division by 2 (pulse travels to seabed and returns) **[1]**
- Correct answer: depth $= 180$ m **[1]**

**[3]**

---

**(a)(ii)**

- Medical use: **prenatal (foetal) scanning** (accept: kidney/gallstone imaging, measuring organ sizes, physiotherapy / breaking up kidney stones). **[1]**
- Reason preferred over X-rays: ultrasound is **non-ionising** — it does not damage DNA / cells / tissue, making it safe for use with pregnant women and developing foetuses (whereas X-rays are ionising radiation and can damage cells). **[1]**

**[2]**

---

**(b)** Award **1 mark each**:

- Diffraction is greatest when the **wavelength is similar to (or larger than) the size of the gap or obstacle**. **[1]**
- High-frequency ultrasound has a **very short wavelength** ($\lambda = v/f$; high $f$ → small $\lambda$). Short wavelengths diffract very little around large structures, meaning the beam does not spread around obstacles effectively — it gives poor coverage of large, deep objects. Additionally, higher-frequency (shorter-wavelength) ultrasound is absorbed more quickly by tissue, reducing penetration depth. **[1]**
- Therefore, lower-frequency (longer-wavelength) ultrasound is used for imaging larger or deeper structures, despite giving lower resolution. **[1]**

**[3]**
