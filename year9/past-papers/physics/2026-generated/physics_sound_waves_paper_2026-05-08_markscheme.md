## Question 1 — Nature of Waves (14 marks)

**Q1(a)** [2 marks]

Transverse: particles (medium) vibrate **perpendicular (at right angles)** to the direction of energy transfer / wave travel. [1]

Longitudinal: particles (medium) vibrate **parallel (along the same direction)** as the direction of energy transfer / wave travel. [1]

---

**Q1(b)** [3 marks — 1 mark each]

| Wave | Answer |
|---|---|
| Sound in air | Longitudinal |
| Light | Transverse |
| Seismic P-wave | Longitudinal |

---

**Q1(c)** [4 marks — 1 mark each]

**(c)(i)** The maximum displacement of a particle from its rest/equilibrium position; or the height of a crest (or depth of a trough) from the midline.

**(c)(ii)** The distance between two successive points that are in phase (e.g. crest to crest, or compression to compression).

**(c)(iii)** The number of complete waves (oscillations/cycles) that pass a fixed point per second. Unit: Hz (hertz).

**(c)(iv)** The time taken for one complete wave (one full oscillation/cycle) to pass a fixed point. Unit: s (seconds).

---

**Q1(d)(i)** [2 marks]

$$T = \frac{1}{f} = \frac{1}{400} = 0.0025 \text{ s}$$

- Correct substitution [1]
- Answer: **0.0025 s** (or $2.5 \times 10^{-3}$ s) with unit [1]

---

**Q1(d)(ii)** [2 marks]

$$\lambda = \frac{v}{f} = \frac{320}{400} = 0.80 \text{ m}$$

- Correct rearrangement and substitution [1]
- Answer: **0.80 m** with unit [1]

---

**Q1(e)** [1 mark]

In one second, 5 complete waves pass, each of length 2.4 m, so the wave travels $5 \times 2.4 = 12$ m per second. This is precisely what the wave equation gives: $v = f\lambda = 5 \times 2.4$.

- $v = 5 \times 2.4 =$ **12 m s$^{-1}$** [1]
- Accept explanation that speed = (number of waves per second) × (length of one wave).

## Question 2 — Sound Waves: Production, Transmission and Speed (14 marks)

**Q2(a)** [3 marks]

Award 1 mark each for any three of the following (max 3):

- The loudspeaker cone vibrates / moves back and forth. [1]
- When the cone moves forward, it pushes air particles together, creating a **compression** (region of higher pressure/density). [1]
- When the cone moves back, it pulls air particles apart, creating a **rarefaction** (region of lower pressure/density). [1]
- These compressions and rarefactions travel outwards from the source, transferring energy through the medium. [1]
- (The particles themselves do not travel with the wave — they oscillate about their rest positions.) [bonus context, not required]

---

**Q2(b)** [2 marks]

- Sound requires a medium (particles) to travel through; it cannot travel without particles. [1]
- A vacuum contains no particles / no matter, so there is nothing to vibrate/transmit the compressions and rarefactions. [1]

---

**Q2(c)(i)** [1 mark]

Sound travels faster in denser/more rigid materials; speed increases from gas → liquid → solid. [1]
- Accept: "The speed of sound is greater in solids than in liquids, and greater in liquids than in gases."

---

**Q2(c)(ii)** [2 marks]

- In steel, the particles (atoms/ions) are much more closely packed / bonded more strongly together. [1]
- They can pass on (transmit) vibrations to their neighbours more quickly, so the sound travels faster. [1]

**Q6(e)** [4 marks]

Award 1 mark each for any four of the following points:

- Higher frequency means **shorter wavelength** (from $v = f\lambda$; at constant $v$, as $f$ increases, $\lambda$ decreases). [1]
- Shorter wavelength allows finer/smaller features to be resolved — gives **better resolution** (the wave can "detect" objects close to the size of the wavelength). [1]
- However, higher-frequency (shorter-wavelength) ultrasound is **absorbed more strongly** by tissue as it travels deeper, so less energy reaches deep structures and the returning echo is too weak to detect. [1]
- Lower-frequency ultrasound penetrates deeper but has poorer resolution (larger wavelength means it cannot resolve fine detail). [1]
- Therefore in practice, the frequency is chosen as a **compromise** between penetration depth and resolution needed for the clinical application. [1]

Standing wave: a wave pattern formed by the superposition of two waves of the same frequency travelling in opposite directions, resulting in points of zero displacement (nodes) and points of maximum displacement (antinodes) that do not move along the medium. [1]

Difference (any one of):
- A standing wave does not transfer energy along the medium (no net energy transport), whereas a progressive wave does. [1]
- In a standing wave, different points have different amplitudes (nodes have zero amplitude, antinodes have maximum amplitude); in a progressive wave, all points have the same amplitude. [1]
- In a standing wave, all particles between two adjacent nodes oscillate in phase; in a progressive wave there is a phase difference between particles at different positions. [1]

*Award 1 mark for a correct description of a standing wave, 1 mark for a valid difference.*

## Question 8 — Extended Response: Wave Behaviour and Applications (10 marks)

**Q8(a)(i)** [1 mark]

Diffraction is greatest when the **wavelength is approximately equal to (or larger than) the size of the gap/obstacle**. [1]
- Accept: "when the gap width is similar to the wavelength."

---

**Q8(a)(ii)** [3 marks]

Calculate (or compare) wavelengths:
- $\lambda_{100} = \frac{340}{100} = 3.4$ m; $\lambda_{4000} = \frac{340}{4000} = 0.085$ m.

Award marks for:
- The low-frequency note (100 Hz) has a much larger wavelength (~3.4 m), which is comparable to or larger than the doorway width (~1 m), so it diffracts significantly around the door frame and is heard clearly around the corner. [1]
- The high-frequency note (4000 Hz) has a much smaller wavelength (~0.085 m), which is much smaller than the gap, so it diffracts very little and travels mainly in a straight line through the doorway — it would not be heard clearly around the corner. [1]
- Therefore the **low-pitched note (100 Hz)** would be heard more clearly around the corner. [1]

---

**Q8(b)** [6 marks]

*Level-marked question: award 1 mark per valid, distinct point up to a maximum of 6.*

Equipment and set-up:
- Two microphones (M1 and M2) placed at a known distance apart (e.g. 1 m) along a straight line from a sound source (loudspeaker or clapper/clap). [1]
- Both microphones connected to a dual-channel oscilloscope (or a data logger / computer interface). [1]

Measurements taken:
- Measure the distance $d$ between the two microphones with a metre rule/tape measure. [1]
- On the oscilloscope, observe the two traces and measure the **time delay** $\Delta t$ between the moment the sound reaches M1 and the moment it reaches M2 (the horizontal offset between the two traces). [1]

Calculating speed of sound:
- Speed of sound $v = \dfrac{d}{\Delta t}$, where $d$ is the separation of the microphones and $\Delta t$ is the time delay read from the oscilloscope. [1]

Source of error and reduction:
- Any one of: reaction time in triggering is eliminated because the oscilloscope records the signals automatically / electronically. [this confirms the method avoids human timing error]
- The distance between the microphones may be measured inaccurately — reduce by using a tape measure carefully, taking multiple measurements and averaging. [1]
- The sound source may not be directly in line with both microphones — ensure alignment carefully. [1]
- Background noise may distort the waveform — carry out the experiment in a quiet room. [1]
- Repeat measurements and average to reduce random error. [1]

*Award up to 6 from the points listed. Quality of explanation is taken into account — a vague point scores 0.*
