### Question 11 (8 marks)

**(a)** **46 g** per 100 g of water **[1]**

**(b)(i)** Mass of KNO₃ in 250 g water at 70 °C:

Solubility at 70 °C = 138 g/100 g water

Mass = (138 / 100) × 250 = **345 g** **[2]**

*(Award 1 mark for correct method even if arithmetic error)*

**(b)(ii)** Mass remaining dissolved at 30 °C:

Solubility at 30 °C = 46 g/100 g → in 250 g water = (46/100) × 250 = 115 g

Mass of crystals = 345 − 115 = **230 g** **[2]**

*(Award 1 mark for correct subtraction method; 1 mark for correct final answer)*

**(c)** Any two from: **[2]**
- As temperature decreases, the maximum amount of solute the solvent can dissolve decreases (solubility decreases)
- The solution becomes supersaturated / contains more dissolved solute than the solubility allows
- Excess solute can no longer remain in solution and comes out of solution as solid crystals
- Particles slow down / have less kinetic energy, so attractive forces between solute particles overcome the interaction with solvent particles, causing them to form an ordered lattice (crystal)

**(d)** From Table 1: at **50 °C**, both KNO₃ and NH₄Cl have a solubility of approximately **85–51 g** — they do not share an exact value in the table.

> **Examiner note:** KNO₃ and NH₄Cl do not share an exact crossover point in the table as provided. Accept the answer that the curves would cross **between 40 °C and 50 °C** where KNO₃ overtakes NH₄Cl (KNO₃: 64 at 40°C < NH₄Cl: 46 — wait this is wrong).

> Checking values: KNO₃ (64) vs NH₄Cl (46) at 40°C → KNO₃ > NH₄Cl throughout. At 0°C: KNO₃ 13 < NH₄Cl 29. So they cross **between 0 °C and 10 °C**.
> At 0°C: KNO₃ = 13, NH₄Cl = 29. At 10°C: KNO₃ = 21, NH₄Cl = 33. They have not crossed by 10°C.
> At 20°C: KNO₃ = 32, NH₄Cl = 38. Still NH₄Cl > KNO₃.
> At 30°C: KNO₃ = 46, NH₄Cl = 42. **KNO₃ overtakes NH₄Cl between 20°C and 30°C.**

**Correct answer:** The curves cross **between 20 °C and 30 °C**, where both salts have a solubility of approximately **40–42 g** per 100 g of water. **[1]**

*(Accept any temperature between 20 °C and 30 °C with an appropriate solubility value)*

### Question 12 (6 marks)

**(a)** Solubility of KNO₃ at 80 °C = 170 g/100 g water. The student's solution contains 120 g per 100 g water. Since 120 g < 170 g, the solution is **unsaturated**. **[2]**

*(1 mark for correct classification; 1 mark for correct comparison using Table 1 data)*

**(b)** Crystals formed on cooling to 20 °C:

Solubility at 20 °C = 32 g/100 g water → max dissolved in 100 g water = 32 g

Crystals = 120 − 32 = **88 g** **[2]**

*(1 mark for method; 1 mark for correct answer)*

**(c)** Any one from: **[1]**
- Wash the crystals with a small amount of cold distilled water (to dissolve surface impurities without re-dissolving the crystals)
- Re-crystallise the product (dissolve crystals again in hot water, filter to remove insoluble impurities, re-cool)

**(d)** Cool the solution **very slowly** / leave to cool over a longer time period — this allows crystals to grow larger and more slowly around fewer nuclei. **[1]**

### Question 13 (5 marks)

**(a)** Any two from: **[2]**
- CO₂ solubility **decreases** with increasing temperature, whereas KNO₃ solubility **increases** with temperature
- The trends are opposite
- KNO₃ shows a large increase in solubility (13 g at 0°C to 170 g at 80°C), while CO₂ shows a decrease (0.35 g at 15°C to 0.15 g at 40°C)
- Both the magnitude and direction of the temperature–solubility relationship differ

**(b)** Any two from: **[2]**
- At higher temperatures, CO₂ gas particles have **greater kinetic energy**
- They move faster and can **overcome the attractions** between the gas and water molecules
- Gas particles **escape from the solution** into the gas phase above the liquid
- The equilibrium between dissolved and undissolved CO₂ shifts towards less dissolved gas

**(c)** Any one from: **[1]**
- Aquatic animals (e.g. fish) receive less dissolved oxygen in warmer water, reducing their survival / causing suffocation
- Warm water near industrial discharge zones has less oxygen, reducing biodiversity
- Ocean warming reduces CO₂ absorption, worsening climate change / ocean acidification decreases

### Question 14 (6 marks)

**(a)** A saturated solution is one that contains the **maximum amount of dissolved solute** at a given temperature (in equilibrium with undissolved solute). **[1]**

*(Must refer to maximum / cannot dissolve more at that temperature)*

**(b)(i)** Rapid / sudden crystallisation is observed — solid crystals appear quickly throughout the solution / the solution turns cloudy and solid forms rapidly. **[1]**

**(b)(ii)** Any two from: **[2]**
- The supersaturated solution contains more dissolved solute than the equilibrium (saturation) concentration allows at that temperature
- The seed crystal provides a **surface / nucleus** for crystallisation to begin
- Dissolved solute particles arrange themselves around the seed crystal in an ordered lattice
- The system moves towards equilibrium by depositing the excess solute as crystals

**(c)** Solubility of NaCl at 20 °C = 36 g/100 g water. The solution contains only 30 g per 100 g water. Since 30 g < 36 g (the maximum), the solution is **unsaturated**. **[2]**

*(1 mark for correct classification; 1 mark for correct comparison with Table 1 value)*
