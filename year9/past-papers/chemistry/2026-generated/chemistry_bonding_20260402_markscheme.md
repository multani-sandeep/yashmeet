### Question 11 — Ionic Bonding (10 marks)

**(a)** The (strong) electrostatic force of attraction between **oppositely charged ions** (cations and anions) in a **giant ionic lattice**. **[1]**

*(Must mention: electrostatic attraction AND oppositely charged ions)*

**(b)** Dot-and-cross diagram for MgO:

- Mg²⁺ ion: shown as [Mg]²⁺ with empty outer shell (2, 8 configuration — show 8 electrons in outer shell of the ion, or simply label Mg²⁺) **[1]**
- O²⁻ ion: shown as [O]²⁻ with 8 electrons in the outer shell (2, 8) **[1]**
- Correct charges shown on both ions **[1]**

*Examiner note: Expect two circles/squares — one representing Mg²⁺ (with ×× symbols for Mg electrons where needed, all moved to O), one representing O²⁻ with 8 electrons in outer shell. Award marks for showing electron transfer clearly, correct electronic configurations of resulting ions, and charges labelled.*

**[3]**

**(c)**
- In **solid** NaCl, ions are held in a fixed lattice and **cannot move** → no electrical conduction. **[1]**
- When **molten**, the lattice breaks down and **ions are free to move** and carry charge → conducts electricity. **[1]**

**[2]**

**(d)** 
- Going from NaF to NaBr, the halide ion increases in size (F⁻ < Cl⁻ < Br⁻). **[1]**
- As the anion gets larger, the distance between the Na⁺ ion and the halide ion increases, **reducing the strength of the electrostatic attraction**. Less energy is needed to break the lattice → melting point decreases. **[1]**

**[2]**

**(e)**
- When a force is applied, a layer of ions shifts relative to the adjacent layer. **[1]**
- Ions of the same charge come to face each other (e.g. Na⁺ faces Na⁺), causing **electrostatic repulsion** that pushes the layers apart and causes the crystal to fracture/shatter. **[1]**

### Question 12 — Metallic Bonding & Groups 1 and 2 (10 marks)

**(a)** 
- A **giant lattice** of positive metal ions (cations) **[1]**
- Surrounded by a **sea of delocalised (free-moving) electrons** that are attracted to the positive ions. **[1]**

**[2]**

**(b)(i)** The strong attraction between the positive metal ions and the sea of delocalised electrons requires a large amount of energy to overcome → high melting point. **[1]**

**(b)(ii)** The delocalised electrons can move freely through the metal lattice and carry charge from one end to the other → metals are good electrical conductors. **[1]**

**(b)(iii)** When a force is applied, layers of metal ions can slide past each other. The delocalised electrons move with the ions, maintaining the metallic bonds throughout → the structure does not shatter → metals are malleable. **[1]**

**(c)(i)** potassium + water → potassium hydroxide + hydrogen **[1]**

*(Accept the balanced symbol equation: 2K(s) + 2H₂O(l) → 2KOH(aq) + H₂(g))*

**(c)(ii)**
- Reactivity **increases** going down Group 1. **[1]**
- Each successive element has its outer electron in a higher energy shell, further from the nucleus. The nuclear attraction on the outer electron decreases (greater shielding by inner electrons). The outer electron is therefore **more easily lost**, making the metal more reactive. **[1]**

**[2]**

**(d)** Ca(s) + 2HCl(aq) → CaCl₂(aq) + H₂(g) **[2]**

*(1 mark for correct formulae and products; 1 mark for correct balancing AND state symbols)*

### Question 13 — Covalent Bonding (10 marks)

**(a)(i)** H₂O dot-and-cross: **[1]**

- Central O atom with 6 outer electrons; 2 H atoms each sharing 1 pair of electrons with O
- O has 2 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs in outer shell (shown as ×× symbols vs • symbols)
- Two O–H single bonds clearly shown

**(a)(ii)** N₂ dot-and-cross: **[2]**

- Each N atom has 5 outer electrons
- **Three** shared pairs of electrons between the two N atoms (triple bond)
- Each N has 1 lone pair remaining
- Award 1 mark for triple bond shown; 1 mark for correct lone pairs on each N

**(a)(iii)** CO₂ dot-and-cross: **[2]**

- Central C atom (4 outer electrons) with 2 O atoms on either side
- Each C–O bond is a **double bond** (2 shared pairs)
- Each O has 2 lone pairs
- Award 1 mark for both double bonds correctly shown; 1 mark for correct lone pairs on O atoms

**(b)** 
- Within iodine molecules, the covalent bonds are **strong**, but the forces **between** molecules (**intermolecular/van der Waals forces**) are weak. **[1]**
- Only a small amount of energy is needed to overcome these weak intermolecular forces → low melting point. The strong I–I covalent bonds are **not** broken on melting. **[1]**

**[2]**

**(c)**
- In **graphite**, each carbon forms only **3 covalent bonds**, leaving one electron per carbon **delocalised** (free to move between layers). These delocalised electrons can carry charge → conducts electricity. **[1]**
- In **diamond**, each carbon forms **4 covalent bonds** in a tetrahedral arrangement. All electrons are used in bonding — there are **no delocalised/free electrons** → cannot conduct electricity. **[1]**

**[2]**

**(d)** Going down Group VII (F₂ → Cl₂ → Br₂ → I₂), the molecules have **more electrons and are larger**. Larger molecules have stronger **van der Waals/intermolecular forces** between them. More energy is needed to overcome these forces → higher boiling points → physical state changes from gas → liquid → solid. **[1]**
