## Core Exam Skill 1 — Source Inference (Paper 1 / Paper 3)

**Question type:** "What can you infer from Source A about X?" (4 marks)

This tests the ability to read **between the lines** — not just what the source says, but what it implies or suggests.

**Structure for each inference:**
> "I can infer that [point]. This is suggested by [specific detail from the source], which implies [the deeper meaning]."

Write two developed inferences. Do not paraphrase — always explain what the detail suggests beyond its surface meaning.

**Weak response:**
"I can infer that soldiers suffered in the trenches. The source says 'the conditions were terrible'."

**Strong response:**
"I can infer that the physical conditions on the Western Front caused severe long-term damage to soldiers' health. The source describes soldiers 'wading through mud above the knee', which implies the constant damp and cold would have led to medical conditions such as trench foot, beyond the immediate danger of combat."

Practice: take any primary source from Y9 topics and write two developed inferences in the format above.

## Core Exam Skill 2 — Source Utility (Paper 1)

**Question type:** "How useful are Sources A and B for an enquiry into X?" (8 marks)

**Common mistake:** students list what the sources say and call that "useful". Examiners want analysis of *why* the source is useful AND what limits its usefulness.

**Framework for each source:**

**Content:** What does the source say that is useful for this enquiry? Explain why those details are helpful.

**Provenance:** Consider Nature (what type of source is it?), Origin (who produced it, when?), Purpose (why was it created?).
- A government poster produced during wartime has a clear propaganda purpose → may exaggerate or omit negative information → limits its reliability but makes it useful as evidence of what the government wanted people to believe

**Own knowledge:** add a brief relevant fact that puts the source in context or supports/challenges it.

**Structure:**
> "Source A is useful for an enquiry into X because it shows [content point]. [Quote]. This is useful because... However, its usefulness is limited because [provenance limitation]. Despite this, the source is still valuable as evidence of [what it does tell us]."

Do the same for Source B, then write a brief overall judgement: which source is MORE useful and why.

## Core Exam Skill 3 — Narrative Account (Paper 1 / Paper 2)

**Question type:** "Write a narrative account analysing [event]" (8 marks)

This tests the ability to tell a structured story that explains cause and consequence — not just list events in order.

**Key requirements:**
1. **Causation:** explain *why* each event happened (link to prior events)
2. **Consequence:** explain what *resulted* from each event
3. **Chronological development:** events must build on each other logically
4. Cover at least **three events or developments**
5. Use connective language: "As a result of... this led to... consequently... this meant that... because of..."

**Weak response:** "First X happened. Then Y happened. Then Z happened."

**Strong response:** "The [event A] resulted in [consequence], which directly led to [event B]. This was significant because [explanation]. As tensions escalated, [event C] occurred, which further [consequence] and ultimately [final consequence]."

## Core Exam Skill 4 — 12-Mark Essay

**Question type:** "Explain why [event/factor] happened. You may use [stimulus A] and [stimulus B] and your own knowledge." (12 marks)

**Structure:**
- Three developed paragraphs, each explaining one cause
- Each paragraph: State the cause → Explain the mechanism → Use specific evidence → Connect to the question
- No need for an introduction or conclusion at 12 marks — use the time for developed points instead
- Use the stimulus points as a springboard, but add your own knowledge to develop them further

**Paragraph structure (PEEL):**
> Point: One reason why [event] happened was [cause].
> Evidence: [specific fact, date, name, statistic].
> Explanation: This led to [event] because [mechanism — how did this cause lead to this result?].
> Link: This was the [most/an equally] significant reason because...

## Core Exam Skill 5 — 20-Mark Essay

**Question type:** "How far do you agree that [statement]?" (16–20 marks)

This is the top-mark essay — it requires a **balanced argument** and a **substantiated judgement**.

**Structure:**
- **Introduction:** briefly state your overall argument (agree / partially agree / disagree)
- **Paragraphs agreeing:** 2–3 points with evidence supporting the statement
- **Paragraphs disagreeing / qualifying:** 2–3 alternative factors or counterpoints
- **Conclusion:** reach a **clear judgement** — which factor was most significant and why? Must go beyond restating the argument — explain the relative importance.

**Common mistakes:**
- Presenting all arguments on one side then all on the other — interweave instead to show analytical thinking
- Weak conclusion: "In conclusion, both factors were important." — this scores low. State a clear hierarchy: which factor mattered most and why.
- Narrative (storytelling) instead of analysis — every paragraph must answer "how far" not just "what happened"

## Y9 Content — Topic Summaries for Practice

### Western Front (1914–18)
Key themes: conditions in the trenches; medical advances (X-rays, blood transfusion, shell shock treatment, plastic surgery, RAMC); Battle of the Somme (1916); significance of the Tank

Practice question: "How useful are Sources A and B for an enquiry into medical conditions on the Western Front?"

---

### Russian Revolution and Stalin
Key themes: why the Tsar fell (WWI, Bloody Sunday, Rasputin, February Revolution); Lenin and the Bolsheviks (October Revolution, Treaty of Brest-Litovsk, Civil War); Stalin's rise to power; collectivisation; purges and the gulags; propaganda and the cult of personality

Practice question: "How far do you agree that Stalin's use of terror was the most important reason for his control of the USSR by 1939?"

---

### Civil Rights in the USA (1945–1965)
Key themes: segregation (Jim Crow laws); Brown v Board of Education (1954); Montgomery Bus Boycott (1955–56); sit-ins and Freedom Rides; March on Washington and MLK's "I Have a Dream" speech (1963); Civil Rights Act (1964); role of Malcolm X and SNCC as alternative approaches

Practice question: "Write a narrative account analysing how the civil rights movement won legal victories between 1954 and 1965."
