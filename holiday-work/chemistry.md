## Y9 Consolidation

### Bonding Types — Quick Reference

| Type | Between | Structure | Properties |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Ionic** | Metal + non-metal | Giant lattice of ions | High MP/BP; conducts when molten or dissolved; brittle |
| **Covalent (simple molecular)** | Non-metal + non-metal | Small molecules | Low MP/BP; doesn't conduct; e.g. H₂O, CO₂, NH₃ |
| **Covalent (giant)** | Non-metal atoms | Massive network | Very high MP/BP; hard; diamond, graphite, SiO₂ |
| **Metallic** | Metal atoms | Positive ions in sea of delocalised electrons | Good conductor; malleable; high MP/BP |

Key point: conductivity explains structure. Ionic conducts when dissolved (ions free to move). Metallic conducts always (delocalised electrons). Simple covalent never conducts (no ions or free electrons).

### Quantitative Chemistry — Moles (just covered in Y9)

This is the most important calculation topic in IGCSE Chemistry. Must be confident before Y10.

**Mole formula triangle:**
```
     mass (g)
    ──────────
  moles × Mr
```
- n = m / Mr (moles = mass ÷ relative formula mass)
- m = n × Mr
- Mr = m / n

**Relative formula mass (Mr):** add up all atomic masses in the formula
- H₂O: (2 × 1) + 16 = 18
- CaCO₃: 40 + 12 + (3 × 16) = 100
- H₂SO₄: (2 × 1) + 32 + (4 × 16) = 98

**Reacting masses:** use moles as the bridge between masses in an equation

Example: How much CaO forms when 50 g of CaCO₃ decomposes?
CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂
1 mol CaCO₃ → 1 mol CaO
n(CaCO₃) = 50/100 = 0.5 mol → n(CaO) = 0.5 mol → m(CaO) = 0.5 × 56 = 28 g

**Percentage composition:** % of an element = (total mass of element / Mr) × 100

**Empirical formula:** simplest ratio of atoms in a compound
Method: mass → moles → divide by smallest → ratio of atoms → formula

### Acids, Bases, and Salts
Key definitions:
- **Acid:** substance that produces H⁺ ions in solution; pH < 7
- **Base:** substance that neutralises an acid; metal oxide or hydroxide
- **Alkali:** a base soluble in water; produces OH⁻ ions; pH > 7

**Neutralisation:** H⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq) → H₂O(l)

**Making salts:**
- Acid + metal → salt + hydrogen gas
- Acid + metal oxide/hydroxide → salt + water (neutralisation)
- Acid + carbonate → salt + water + carbon dioxide
- Acid + ammonia solution → ammonium salt

**Naming salts:** hydrochloric acid → chloride; sulfuric acid → sulfate; nitric acid → nitrate

**Indicators:** litmus (red in acid, blue in alkali); universal indicator (full pH range); pH meter (most accurate)

### Rates of Reaction
Rate = amount of product formed (or reactant used) ÷ time

**Factors that increase rate:**
1. **Temperature** — molecules move faster → more frequent, more energetic collisions
2. **Concentration (solutions) or pressure (gases)** — more particles per unit volume → more frequent collisions
3. **Surface area** — more exposed particles → more collisions possible
4. **Catalyst** — provides alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy → more particles have enough energy to react

**Measuring rate:**
- Volume of gas produced per second (use gas syringe or water displacement)
- Change in mass over time (if gas escapes — use a balance)
- Change in colour or turbidity (use colorimeter or visual)

**Collision theory:** for a reaction to occur, particles must collide with sufficient energy (activation energy) and correct orientation.

**Activation energy:** minimum energy required for a reaction. A catalyst lowers it but does not change itself.

Graphs to recognise: rate vs concentration (linear for simple reactions); rate vs temperature (exponential); mass lost vs time (decreasing curve reaching a flat line when reaction complete).

### Electrochemistry — Electrolysis
**Electrolysis:** using electricity to decompose an ionic compound.

**Requirements:**
- An electrolyte: ionic compound that is either molten or dissolved in water (so ions are free to move)
- Two electrodes: cathode (−, reduction) and anode (+, oxidation)

**At the cathode (−):** positive ions (cations) gain electrons → reduced
- Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (copper deposited)
- H⁺ + e⁻ → ½H₂ (hydrogen gas produced from water)

**At the anode (+):** negative ions (anions) lose electrons → oxidised
- 2Cl⁻ → Cl₂ + 2e⁻ (chlorine gas from concentrated HCl solution)
- 2H₂O → O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ (oxygen gas from dilute solutions)

**Which product forms at the anode from aqueous solution?**
- Concentrated halide solution → halogen gas (Cl₂, Br₂)
- Dilute solution → oxygen gas

**Applications:** purifying copper; electroplating; extracting aluminium from molite

### Organic Chemistry
**Homologous series:** a family of compounds with the same functional group and general formula.

**Alkanes** — general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂; saturated; single C−C bonds only
- Methane CH₄, ethane C₂H₆, propane C₃H₈, butane C₄H₁₀
- Used as fuels; combustion: CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

**Alkenes** — general formula CₙH₂ₙ; unsaturated; contain C=C double bond
- Ethene C₂H₄, propene C₃H₆
- Test: decolourise bromine water (addition reaction)
- Addition reactions: X₂, H₂, H₂O can add across the double bond

**Polymers:** alkenes undergo addition polymerisation
- Many ethene molecules (monomers) → polyethene (polymer)
- Repeat unit shown in brackets with n subscript

**Cracking:** breaking long-chain alkanes into shorter, more useful alkenes and alkanes by high temperature and a catalyst.

---

### Metals and the Reactivity Series

Reactivity series (most reactive to least): K · Na · Ca · Mg · Al · Zn · Fe · Ni · Sn · Pb · Cu · Ag · Au · Pt

**Displacement reactions:** a more reactive metal displaces a less reactive one from its compound
- Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu (zinc displaces copper)

**Extraction of metals:**
- Reactive metals (Al, Mg, Na): extracted by electrolysis
- Moderately reactive metals (Zn, Fe): extracted by reduction with carbon/coke
- Unreactive metals (Cu, Ag, Au): found native or easily reduced

**Iron and steel:** iron from blast furnace; carbon is removed and other elements added to make steel alloys.
