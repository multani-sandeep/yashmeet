## The PEA+ Framework

All analytical writing at GCSE must follow Point → Evidence → Analysis + (effect, purpose, or context).

**Weak response (what to avoid):**
"The writer uses the word 'cold' to describe the room. This shows it is cold."

**Strong PEA+ response:**
"The writer's choice of 'cold' to describe the room creates an atmosphere of emotional isolation, suggesting the character feels unloved and unwelcome in what should be their home. The word choice also foreshadows the rejection that follows in the next paragraph."

Every analytical paragraph must:
1. Make a **clear point** (what is the writer doing?)
2. Embed a **specific quotation** — not a block quote
3. Analyse the **effect or purpose** — not just what it means, but why it matters and how it affects the reader
4. Where possible, link to **structure, context, or the writer's wider purpose**

## Language Paper 1 — Fiction

### Q2: Language Analysis (8 marks)
**Task:** How does the writer use language to describe X?

Method:
1. Identify 3–4 language features (imagery, word choice, sentence structure, tone, personification, etc.)
2. For each: name the feature → quote it → analyse its effect on the reader
3. Vary vocabulary: "the writer suggests… conveys… implies… reinforces…"

Common features to look for and name accurately:
- Simile / metaphor / extended metaphor / personification
- Connotations of a word choice
- Sibilance, alliteration, or other sound effects
- Short sentences (pace, shock) vs long sentences (building tension)
- Repetition (emphasis)
- Listing / tripling (overwhelm, intensity)

Practice: Take any extract (newspaper article, opening of a novel) and write one full Q2 response in 12 minutes.

---

### Q3: Structure Analysis (8 marks)
**Task:** How has the writer structured the text to interest you as a reader?

Think about **whole-text structure** — not just language:
- Where does the text start and where does it end? (What has changed?)
- What is revealed early vs late? (What is withheld for tension?)
- How does the focus shift? (Zooming in/out, wide shot to close-up)
- Where is the turning point?
- How does the opening hook the reader? How does the ending resolve or leave questions?
- Paragraph lengths — short for pace, long for reflection?

Structure vocabulary: exposition · build-up · climax · resolution · circular structure · in medias res · flashback · linear narrative · non-linear

---

### Q4: Evaluation (20 marks)
**Task:** A student says "The writer makes the character seem [X]." To what extent do you agree?

This is the highest-mark Language question. Structure:
- **Agree** with specific evidence and analysis (2–3 points)
- **Partially agree / nuance** — moments where the opposite is true (1–2 points)
- **Overall judgement** — what is the dominant impression and why?

Do not just agree. Do not just disagree. The mark scheme rewards **balanced evaluation with a clear personal judgement** backed by evidence.

### Q5: Creative Writing (40 marks)
Either descriptive writing or narrative writing (or a choice of both).

**Descriptive writing — key techniques:**
- Use all five senses — but don't list them mechanically
- "Show, don't tell" — create atmosphere through specific detail, not adjectives
- Vary sentence structure for effect: short for impact, longer for building atmosphere
- Use an unusual or unexpected angle (e.g. describe a scene from an unexpected perspective)
- Strong opening sentence and strong closing sentence

**Narrative writing — structure:**
- A clear arc: opening → build-up → complication → climax → resolution
- Developed characters — show character through action and dialogue, not description
- Control of pace — slow down for key moments, speed up for action
- Avoid clichés: "dark and stormy night", "heart pounding"

**Common mistakes to avoid:**
- Over-relying on adjectives instead of precise nouns and verbs
- Rushing the ending — spend as much time on resolution as on opening
- Forgetting to vary sentence length
- Losing the narrative thread by adding too many characters or ideas

Practice: One Q5 response per week, timed to 45 minutes. Peer review against the mark scheme criteria.

## Language Paper 2 — Non-Fiction

### Q2: Summary (8 marks)
**Task:** Summarise differences (or similarities) between Source A and Source B on a given topic.

Method:
- Identify 4 clear points of difference
- Use one piece of evidence from each source per point
- Write in continuous prose — not bullet points
- Avoid quoting large sections; paraphrase and embed short phrases

Do not: analyse language here. This is a summary only.

---

### Q3: Language Analysis of Non-Fiction (12 marks)
Same skills as Paper 1 Q2, but the source is non-fiction (article, speech, letter).
Focus on: how the writer positions themselves, tone, persuasive devices, and selection of detail.

Persuasive devices to identify:
- Rhetorical questions (engage reader, imply the answer is obvious)
- Direct address (you / we — inclusive or accusatory)
- Statistics and facts (create authority)
- Anecdote (make abstract personal and relatable)
- Tripling / Rule of three (memorable, emphatic)
- Counter-argument then rebuttal (appears balanced, dismisses opposition)

---

### Q4: Comparison of Writers' Perspectives (16 marks)
**Task:** Compare how writers present their attitudes to X in Source A and Source B.

Structure — use a comparative frame throughout (not "Source A then Source B"):
1. Open with overall statement: "Both writers convey strong opinions about X, though their perspectives differ significantly in…"
2. For each comparison point: state both writers' positions → evidence from both → analyse how each writer constructs their viewpoint
3. Comparative connectives: similarly · in contrast · whereas · while · conversely · unlike · both

---

### Q5: Viewpoint Writing (40 marks)
Write an article, letter, speech, or essay presenting a viewpoint.

Before writing: identify audience, purpose, and form — they shape every choice.

Structure:
- Strong opening: rhetorical question, provocative statement, or vivid anecdote
- 3–4 developed paragraphs, each with one main argument
- Counter-argument acknowledged and rebutted
- Strong conclusion that returns to the opening theme

Register: formal but not stiff; intelligent but accessible.

Practice: One Q5 response per week, 45 minutes.

## Analytical Vocabulary to Build

Avoid repeating "the writer shows" or "this means". Use:
- conveys · implies · suggests · reinforces · contrasts · juxtaposes · evokes · foreshadows · subverts
- the reader is invited to / encouraged to / left feeling
- this reflects / highlights / exposes / challenges
- structurally / linguistically / semantically · the connotations of X suggest…
