---

## Daily Target

**30 minutes every day** — any time of day, but consistently.

Reading quality fiction at this age significantly improves the ability to analyse language, structure, and characterisation in GCSE English. It also expands vocabulary passively, which directly benefits analytical writing.

## Recommended Books (age 14–15, GCSE-relevant)

### Literary Fiction (directly builds GCSE English skills)
- *Never Let Me Go* — Kazuo Ishiguro (themes: identity, mortality, human nature; excellent for literature analysis practice)
- *Of Mice and Men* — John Steinbeck (often a GCSE set text; clear themes, concise, powerful)
- *The Curious Incident of the Dog in the Night-Time* — Mark Haddon (narrative voice, unreliable narrator)
- *Brave New World* — Aldous Huxley (dystopian; complex themes of control, freedom, identity)
- *Lord of the Flies* — William Golding (common GCSE modern text; themes of civilisation vs savagery)

### Non-Fiction (supports Language Paper 2 and analytical thinking)
- *Sapiens: A Brief History of Humankind* — Yuval Noah Harari (abridged or young adult version; excellent for history and science links)
- *Factfulness* — Hans Rosling (data and perspective; challenges assumptions)
- *Bad Science* — Ben Goldacre (critical thinking about evidence; science literacy)

### Science and History (cross-subject reading)
- *The Disappearing Spoon* — Sam Kean (chemistry of the periodic table — entertaining and useful)
- *A Short History of Nearly Everything* — Bill Bryson (broad science from physics to biology)
- *The Diary of a Young Girl* — Anne Frank (primary historical source; also excellent GCSE English material)

## Reading as GCSE Preparation

While reading, practise noticing the following — this trains the analytical eye needed for GCSE English papers:

**Language choices:** When a word or phrase strikes you as unusual, effective, or unexpected, pause and ask: why did the author choose this word? What effect does it create on the reader?

**Structure:** Note where the writer starts and where a chapter or section ends. What has changed? Where is the tension? Where does the pace slow down or speed up?

**Characterisation:** How does the author reveal character — through dialogue, action, description, what others say about them? What is implied rather than stated?

**Themes:** What larger ideas is the text exploring? (power, identity, freedom, belonging, justice, loss)

No formal writing is required — this is observation practice. Occasionally (once per book) discuss one of these with Sandeep: choose a passage and explain what you noticed and why it was effective.

## GCSE Literature Texts

If the school confirms Year 10 set texts before September, begin reading them during the India phase of the trip.

Common Edexcel / AQA GCSE Literature set texts for Y10:
- **Shakespeare:** *Macbeth*, *Romeo and Juliet*, *A Midsummer Night's Dream*, *The Merchant of Venice*
- **Modern prose:** *An Inspector Calls* (Priestley), *Lord of the Flies* (Golding), *Never Let Me Go* (Ishiguro)
- **19th century novel:** *A Christmas Carol* (Dickens), *The Strange Case of Dr Jekyll and Mr Hyde* (Stevenson), *Great Expectations* (Dickens)

If no texts are confirmed yet, *Lord of the Flies* or *Of Mice and Men* are safe bets — both commonly appear on GCSE syllabuses and are short enough to read and re-read.

## Reading Aloud (occasional, with Sandeep)

Once per week, read 1–2 pages aloud from whatever you are reading. This improves:
- Pacing and fluency
- Awareness of punctuation (how a writer uses full stops and commas to control pace)
- Confidence with complex vocabulary

After reading, explain in 2–3 sentences what is happening in that passage and why it matters to the story.
